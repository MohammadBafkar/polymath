# Interview guide: {{title}}

> Rob Fitzpatrick, _The Mom Test_: questions about the past beat questions
> about the future; specific beats general; talk less, listen more.

## Research question

The one thing this round of interviews is trying to answer. Resist the urge
to bundle five questions; pick one.

> {{research_question}}

## Recruit

- Audience: who specifically (role, segment, behavior).
- Screener: 2–3 questions that confirm fit; reject silently.
- Target count: 5–8 conversations.

## Opening (2 min)

- One-line context for the interviewee. Don't tell them what you're hoping
  to find — primes the answer.
- Ask permission to take notes / record.

## Past behavior, not hypothetical (15 min)

Past actions are evidence. Hypothetical preferences are noise.

- "Tell me about the last time you tried to do X."
- "Walk me through what happened the last time you ran into …"
- "What were you doing five minutes before that moment?"
- "Who else was involved? What did they do?"

## Specifics, not generalities (10 min)

- "Show me how you do that today" (screen-share or sketch).
- "What did you use? What tab did you have open? What did you type?"
- "What was the most frustrating part of that specific instance?"
- "What did you do as a workaround?"

## The signal-test questions (5 min)

These are the questions Fitzpatrick says actually predict behavior. Use them.

- "How much time/money does this currently cost you?"
- "What have you already tried?"
- "Who else is involved in solving this?"

## Avoid

- "Would you …?" (hypothetical)
- "Do you think …?" (opinion, not behavior)
- "How important is …?" (rating bias)
- Pitching the product. You're researching, not selling.

## Synthesis

Per-conversation: 3–5 quotes that stuck (verbatim), 1 surprise, 1 confirmed
hypothesis, 1 disconfirmed hypothesis.

Across the set: an affinity-mapping pass. Don't conclude until you've talked
to at least 5 people. Conclusions from N=1 are vibes.
