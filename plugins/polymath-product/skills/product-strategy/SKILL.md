# product-strategy

> Strategy is the set of choices about where to play and how to win — not a feature list with ambition. Make the choices explicit and falsifiable.

## When to use

- A product/segment needs a strategic frame: who it's for, how it's positioned, how it's priced, why it wins, how it launches.
- The user asks about vision, positioning, ICP, pricing/packaging, GTM, or competitive moat.
- A workflow invokes `polymath-product:product-strategy`.

This is the *strategy/market layer*. It is not a feature spec (`polymath-product:prd`), a one-page decision brief (`polymath-communication:exec-brief`), a single research persona (`polymath-research:persona`), or backlog prioritization (`polymath-planning:prioritize`).

## Components (pick what the question needs)

- **Vision** — the change in the world if you win; the 3-year north star, not next quarter.
- **ICP & segmentation** — the specific ideal customer and the segments you are *not* serving (a strategy that serves everyone serves no one).
- **Positioning & messaging** — the alternative you're displacing, your unique value, and the message per segment (Dunford-style: competitive alternatives → unique attributes → value → who cares).
- **Pricing & packaging** — model (seat/usage/tier), value metric, packaging tiers, and the willingness-to-pay logic.
- **Moat** — why this advantage compounds (network effects, switching cost, data, brand, economies of scale), not just "we're first".
- **GTM / launch** — motion (PLG / sales-led / hybrid), channels, launch tiers/timing, and the leading indicator of traction.

## Procedure

1. Clarify the strategic question and scope (whole product vs a bet vs a segment).
2. Work the relevant components above; force explicit *choices* (and the roads not taken).
3. State the **key assumptions** the strategy bets on and how each could be cheaply tested.
4. Define **success metrics** and the leading indicator that tells you early if it's working.
5. Output the strategy: vision, ICP, positioning, pricing, moat, GTM — each a decision, with assumptions + how to validate.

## Quality bar

- Every section is a *choice* with an explicit alternative rejected, not a description.
- ICP names who you are NOT for; positioning names the competitive alternative.
- The moat explains why the advantage compounds, not just that you have one.
- Bets are stated as assumptions with a cheap test, not asserted as fact.
